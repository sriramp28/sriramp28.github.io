About

Built Around Hard Physics.

I am a thermofluids researcher, computational simulation engineer, and educator trained across mechanical engineering, aerospace engineering, applied mathematics, and high-performance scientific computing. My work centers on physical systems where flow, heat, chemistry, acoustics, structure, and scale interactions must be understood together rather than as isolated effects.

I build and analyze numerical models, run large simulations, develop postprocessing algorithms, and turn dense fields into interpretable engineering arguments. I am especially interested in work where rigorous computation informs design judgment, model assessment, thermal-fluid performance, acoustic behavior, and coupled multiphysics response.

I also teach aerodynamics, computational fluid dynamics, fluid mechanics, and computing with the same emphasis on physical interpretation. Whether the topic is lift generation, finite-difference stability, odd-even decoupling in the heat equation, or simulation workflow, I try to help students see both the mathematics and the engineering consequence of a model.

Research identity

From equations to interpretable engineering insight.

My work combines governing-equation modeling, mesh-based discretization, algorithm development, high-performance computation, and diagnostics that convert large simulation outputs into physical arguments. The emphasis is on explaining what mechanisms dominate, where models lose information, and which quantities remain reliable when a system is simplified.

Engineering range

Transferable across engineering systems.

The problems I work on involve transport, thermal gradients, instability, turbulence, acoustic response, reacting mixtures, filtering, and model assessment. The same numerical habits apply broadly across thermal systems, propulsion, energy conversion, process flows, room acoustics, aeroacoustics, fluid-structure interaction, thermo-structural response, and coupled-field simulation.

Communication

From analysis to usable design judgment.

I care about turning complex simulations into decisions that can guide engineering practice. That means nondimensionalization, careful visualization, quantitative comparison, reproducible workflows, and writing that connects diagnostics to physical interpretation, design tradeoffs, mechanical constraints, thermal margins, and model limitations. The same communication style carries into teaching, where numerical artifacts, governing equations, and computed fields have to become ideas students can reason with independently.
